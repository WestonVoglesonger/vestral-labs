The Problem

BCI development is still a custom science project

Not repeatable engineering. Every team builds bespoke infrastructure from scratch, losing months to problems that should have been solved once.

Unrequited Specialization

BCI researchers optimize algorithms in isolation. Each team develops sophisticated signal processing, decoder architectures, and evaluation methods—but none of it transfers. There's no shared performance ground truth.

The result: brilliant work that can't be compared, reproduced, or built upon. Every lab reinvents the same wheels while the field moves slower than it should.

The Idle Paradox

Our research found 49% performance variation attributable to platform effects alone— not algorithm quality, not neural signal differences. Just platform inconsistency.

Five Failure Modes

Where teams lose months

  1. No shared performance ground truth

    What does "90% accuracy" mean? On which dataset? Which preprocessing? Which hardware? Claims are incomparable because there's no standard to compare against.

  2. Inconsistent evaluation methods

    Cross-validation schemes, train/test splits, online vs. offline metrics— every lab does it differently. Published results don't replicate because the evaluation itself isn't reproducible.

  3. Hard-to-compare results

    Even when methods are similar, subtle differences in timing, filtering, and artifact rejection make direct comparison impossible. Good work gets lost because it can't be contextualized.

  4. Safety and validation gaps

    No standard safety envelopes. No shared validation primitives. Each team builds bespoke safety checks—or worse, skips them. Regulatory paths become longer because there's no established baseline.

  5. Interoperability chaos

    Implant A doesn't talk to software B. Wearable C uses a proprietary format. Every integration is a custom project. The ecosystem can't compound because nothing connects.

The Cost

What this means for the industry

Slower Progress

Teams spend 40% of engineering time on infrastructure that should exist. That's time not spent on actual neuroscience.

Wasted Capital

Investors fund the same infrastructure work at multiple companies. Duplicated effort across the industry.

Regulatory Friction

Without standard validation methods, every regulatory submission is a first-of-kind exercise. Approval timelines stretch.

Talent Bottleneck

Skilled engineers leave academia but can't transfer their methods. Institutional knowledge stays siloed.

What should exist

Other engineering disciplines have solved this. Chip design has SPICE. Machine learning has MLPerf. Web development has Lighthouse. Even video games have standardized benchmarks.

BCI should have:

  • Reproducible benchmarks that run on real hardware with standardized datasets and metrics

  • Safety envelopes with validation primitives that accelerate regulatory approval

  • Interoperability schemas so devices and software can actually work together

  • End-to-end measurement for latency and jitter across distributed systems
